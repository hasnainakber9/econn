ECON 511: Economic Analysis for Business Decisions

The Role of Scarcity

Economics is the study of how we make decisions in a world of limited resources (scarcity). Because we can't have everything, we must make choices. In tech, this means a company has scarce resources like engineering hours, server capacity, and capital. They must choose *what* to build.

Opportunity Cost & Trade-offs (PPF)

The **Opportunity Cost** is the value of the next-best alternative you give up when you make a choice. If you spend 100 engineer hours on AI features, you can't spend those same hours on a new mobile app. The lost mobile app development is the opportunity cost.

This trade-off can be shown with a **Production Possibilities Frontier (PPF)**. It shows the maximum possible combinations of two products a company can produce with its given resources.

  • Point A & B: Efficient. All resources are fully used.
  • Point C: Inefficient. Resources are wasted (e.g., engineers on standby).
  • Point D: Unattainable. Not possible with current resources.

Scarcity and Innovation

Innovation (like a new coding framework or AI tool) makes us more productive. It pushes the entire PPF outward, allowing us to produce *more* of both goods with the same resources. This makes "Point D" attainable.

Demand for Digital Products

The **Law of Demand** still applies: as the price of a digital product (like a streaming subscription or software) falls, the quantity demanded generally increases.

Determinants of Demand (Shifts)

These factors can change the *entire* demand for a product at every price, shifting the curve.

  • Consumer Preferences: A product going "viral" or getting a great review shifts demand right.
  • Prices of Related Goods: If a competitor (substitute) raises its price, demand for your product shifts right.
  • Income: For most "normal" goods, as income rises, demand shifts right.

A positive review shifts demand from D1 to D2, leading to a higher equilibrium price (P2) and quantity (Q2).

Supply of Digital Products

This is where digital products are unique. They often have high *fixed costs* (cost to build the first copy) but near-zero *marginal costs* (cost to sell one more copy). This can affect supply dynamics, but the basic model is still used.

Case studies like Hurricane Sandy are often used to analyze market responses to crises and the effect of government policies.

Price Ceilings and Shortages

During a crisis, the demand for essential goods (like water, generators, gas) skyrockets, shifting demand right. This would normally cause prices to rise sharply (price gouging).

To prevent this, governments often impose a **Price Ceiling**, a legal maximum price. While well-intentioned, this creates a **shortage**: at the low, legal price, consumers demand far more (Qd) than suppliers are willing to sell (Qs).

The market equilibrium price is P*. The government sets a Price Ceiling below it. At this low price, Quantity Demanded (Qd) is much larger than Quantity Supplied (Qs), creating a persistent shortage.

Implicit vs. Explicit Costs

To understand a business, we must consider two types of costs:

  • Explicit Costs: Obvious, out-of-pocket payments. (e.g., employee salaries, rent, materials).
  • Implicit Costs: Opportunity costs. What the business owner gives up. (e.g., the $80,000/year salary the owner could be earning at another job).

Accounting Profit vs. Economic Profit

This distinction is critical for decision-making.

  • Accounting Profit = Total Revenue - Explicit Costs
  • Economic Profit = Total Revenue - (Explicit Costs + Implicit Costs)

A firm can have a positive Accounting Profit but a negative Economic Profit, meaning the owner would be better off closing the business and taking the other job.

Total, Fixed, and Variable Costs

Production costs are broken down into:

  • Total Fixed Cost (TFC): Costs that don't change with output (e.g., factory rent).
  • Total Variable Cost (TVC): Costs that *do* change with output (e.g., materials, labor).
  • Total Cost (TC): TC = TFC + TVC

Note how TFC is a flat line, and the TC curve is just the TVC curve shifted up by the amount of TFC.

Market Equilibrium Concept

Definition: Market equilibrium occurs when quantity demanded (Qd) equals quantity supplied (Qs) at a particular price. At this point (P*, Q*), the market is "cleared."

Key Formula: Qd = Qs

Shifts in Supply (e.g., Subsidy)

A government subsidy lowers costs, shifting supply right (S1 to S2), resulting in a lower price and higher quantity.

Real-World Applications (Pakistan Context):
  • Q1: Rising electricity prices → Production costs up → Supply shifts left → Higher prices for goods.
  • Q2: Fertilizer subsidies → Production costs down → Supply shifts right → Lower prices for produce.

Key Production Concepts

Understanding the relationship between inputs (like labor) and outputs (total product).

  • Total Product (TP): Total output produced.
  • Marginal Product (MP): The additional output from one more unit of labor. (ΔTP / ΔL)
  • Average Product (AP): The output per unit of labor. (TP / L)

Marginal and Average Product Curves

Key Relationships & Rules:
  • Q5: When MP > AP, the AP curve is rising.
  • Q7: When AP is at its maximum, MP = AP (the curves intersect).
  • When MP < AP, the AP curve is falling.
  • Q12: The "Law of Diminishing Marginal Returns" begins when the MP curve starts to decline (after Labor = 3 in this graph).

Average and Marginal Cost Curves

These curves show the cost per unit of output. They are a mirror image of the production curves.

  • Marginal Cost (MC): Cost of producing one more unit. (ΔTC / ΔQ)
  • Average Variable Cost (AVC): Variable cost per unit. (TVC / Q)
  • Average Total Cost (ATC): Total cost per unit. (TC / Q)
  • Average Fixed Cost (AFC): Fixed cost per unit. (TFC / Q)
Critical Relationships:
  • Q6: MC and MP are inversely related. When MP rises, MC falls. When MP falls (diminishing returns), MC rises.
  • Q8 & Q13: When MC is below AVC, it pulls the average down (AVC is falling).
  • Q14: The MC curve intersects the AVC and ATC curves at their absolute minimum points.

Long-Run Average Cost (LRAC) Curve

In the long run, all costs are variable (e.g., a firm can build a bigger factory). The LRAC curve shows the lowest possible average cost for any given level of output.

Q15: Economies of Scale (LRAC Falling)

Bigger is cheaper. Doubling inputs more than doubles output.

  • Labor Specialization
  • Efficient Capital
  • Bulk Buying
Q16: Diseconomies of Scale (LRAC Rising)

Bigger is more expensive. The firm becomes too large to manage.

  • Control & Coordination Problems
  • Communication Issues
  • Bureaucracy

Characteristics

A "price taker" market model with many firms, identical products, and easy entry/exit.

  • Q27: The firm's demand curve is perfectly elastic (horizontal) because it must accept the market price. The *industry* demand curve is still downward-sloping.
  • Q29: Firms can't advertise; the product is standardized, so there's nothing to differentiate.

Profit Maximization (TR/TC Approach)

Firms maximize profit at the output level where Total Revenue (TR) exceeds Total Cost (TC) by the largest amount.

Q25: Shutdown Rule (Short-Run)

If Market Price < minimum AVC, the firm should shut down immediately. It loses less money (only its fixed costs) than it would by producing.

Q24 & Q26: Long-Run Equilibrium

Abnormal profits (like in the chart) attract new firms. This increases market supply, which pushes the price down until all firms earn only "normal profit" (where P = ATC).
